\title{\boldmath Electrical Characterisation of Silicon Pad Sensors Irradiated at the Rhode Island Nuclear Reactor for the CMS Endcap Calorimeter Upgrade}


\author[a]{Nick Hinton}
\author[b]{Timo Peltola}
\author[c]{Thorben Quast}
\author[c]{Eva Sicking}

\affiliation[a]{Brown University}
\affiliation[b]{Texas Tech University}
\affiliation[c]{CERN Experimental Physics Department}

\collaboration[d]{on behalf of the CMS HGCAL silicon group}

\emailAdd{thorben.quast@cern.ch}

\abstract{
    Electric characteristics of prototype sensors foreseen for the CMS calorimeter endcap after neutron irradiation at RINSC have been characterised and the empiric findings are presented in this paper.\newline\newline
    20 / 26 good sensors (i.e. passing sensor quality specifications). 
    Other 6 with new discharges during tests and with previous mechanical defects leading to discharges, respectively.\newline\newline
    Based on characterisation of good sensors, all findings match their expectation from previous R$\&$D work on silicon sensors: \newline
    (1) Sensor pad currents scale proportionally with the fluence they are exposed to.\newline 
    (2) Experimental evidence that depletion voltages increase with fluence.\newline
    (3) Annealing by exposing the sensors to \SI{60}{\celsius} for \SI{80}{\min} lowers the leakage current and depletion voltage which is beneficial for the final detector operation.\newline\newline
    This paper does not attempt to provide theoretical explanations for subtle differences in electrical properties (in particular in pad capacitances) between sensor production parameters that have been observed.
}
